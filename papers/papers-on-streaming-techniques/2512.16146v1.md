INTRODUCTION\n\nEvent-streaming architectures have become central to modern computing infrastructures, enabling enterprises to process high-volume data in near real time across use cases such as financial fraud detection, supply-chain monitoring, smart-city telemetry, and personalized recommendations. BACKGROUND ON KAFKA EVENT STREAMING\n\nApache Kafka has become the de facto backbone for modern event-driven architectures, offering a unified framework for high-throughput data ingestion, durable storage, and real-time analytics. Originally designed at LinkedIn to handle continuous user activity streams, Kafka evolved under the Apache Software Foundation into a general-purpose event-streaming platform capable of sustaining millions of messages per second across distributed clusters [12], [15].\n\nDespite its maturity, designing robust Kafka-based systems remains complex. Challenges include maintaining consistency across microservices, handling schema evolution, balancing throughput versus latency, and managing long-term retention. These challenges gave rise to recurring design patterns—such as CQRS, log compaction, and saga orchestration—that abstract proven architectural solutions [5], [6], [9].\n\n&lt;img&gt;Kafka Event-Streaming Architecture Overview&lt;/img&gt;\n\n# Kafka Event-Streaming Architecture Overview\n\nAnother persistent challenge involves benchmarking and reproducibility. Core Architecture and Data Model\n\nKafka organizes data into *topics*, each subdivided into multiple *partitions*. Producers write events to partitions sequentially, while consumers read them in the same order, enabling deterministic replay and horizontal scalability. A distributed coordination mechanism—initially ZooKeeper and now Kafka’s internal *KRaft controller*—manages partition metadata, broker membership, and leader election. Replication ensures fault tolerance, while batching and zero-copy I/O achieve high throughput.\n\nA key abstraction is the *log*, where each event is a time-ordered record identified by an offset. This append-only log model provides immutability and predictable performance. It also enables stream processors, such as Apache Flink, ksqlDB, or Kafka Streams, to perform stateful computations on continuous data without intermediate persistence layers.\n\n## B. Event Processing Semantics\n\nFig. 2. Heatmap-style Venn diagram illustrating common co-usage relationships among Kafka design patterns across 42 reviewed studies.\n\nKafka supports three delivery guarantees—*at-most-once*, *at-least-once*, and *exactly-once*. The introduction of idempotent producers and transactional APIs (since Kafka 0.11) allows developers to build pipelines that maintain end-to-end consistency even under broker failures. Exactly-once semantics (EOS) have been adopted heavily in financial and compliance workloads, where duplicate or lost events are unacceptable [11], [18].\n\n## III. RELATED WORK\n\nSeveral studies have explored event-driven architectures and stream-processing frameworks, but few have synthesized reusable design patterns and benchmarking practices specifically for Kafka. Psaltoglou et al. [5] presented a systematic review of event-driven architectures for big data analytics but treated Kafka only as one of several messaging platforms. Berg [6] proposed stream-processing design patterns at a conceptual level, without empirical validation or benchmarking. Raptis and Passarella [18] provided a recent survey on networked data streaming, focusing primarily on performance modeling. In contrast, this study consolidates architecture-level design patterns, co-usage relationships, and reproducibility gaps across 42 Kafka-focused papers spanning multiple domains. This focus on reproducibility and empirical benchmarking distinguishes it from earlier literature.\n\nIn large-scale deployments, Kafka often operates as an integration layer between OLTP and OLAP systems. For example, *Change Data Capture (CDC)* tools like Debezium stream row-level changes from relational databases into Kafka topics. Downstream consumers can materialize these streams into analytical stores or trigger reactive microservices, enabling near-real-time synchronization across systems.\n\n## C. Recent surveys and experience reports highlight both the breadth of Kafka deployments and the variability in evaluation methods [5]–[7], [9], [18]. This paper addresses that gap by (i) cataloging recurring Kafka design patterns synthesized from academic and industry sources, (ii) analyzing how those patterns are empirically evaluated, and (iii) surfacing reproducibility challenges with practical guidance for architects and researchers.\nThe contributions of this paper are threefold:\n* A lightweight taxonomy of nine recurring Kafka design patterns, summarizing their intent and common deployment domains.\n* An analysis of benchmarking practices across 42 studies, comparing standard suites such as TPCx-Kafka and the Yahoo benchmark with custom workloads, and highlighting configuration gaps.\n* Practical heuristics and tabular summaries to assist with pattern selection and planning reproducible evaluations.\n**Research Questions.** This study is guided by two questions that structure the rest of the paper:\n* RQ1: Which design patterns recur most often in Kafka-based systems?\n* RQ2: How are Kafka-based systems empirically evaluated in the literature?\nThe goal is descriptive: to catalogue existing patterns, their applications and co-usage trends, and to survey how research papers and industrial reports evaluate them.\n## II. BACKGROUND ON KAFKA EVENT STREAMING\nApache Kafka has become the de facto backbone for modern event-driven architectures, offering a unified framework for high-throughput data ingestion, durable storage, and real-time analytics. Originally designed at LinkedIn to handle continuous user activity streams, Kafka evolved under the Apache Software Foundation into a general-purpose event-streaming platform capable of sustaining millions of messages per second across distributed clusters [12], [15].\n\n\n---\n\n\n## Page 2\n\n# Kafka Event-Streaming Architecture Overview\n&lt;img&gt;Kafka Event-Streaming Architecture Overview&lt;/img&gt;\nHigh-level architecture of an Apache Kafka event-streaming ecosystem\nFig. 1. Kafka deployment architecture demonstrating CQRS, CDC, Exactly-Once, and stream enrichment patterns.\n## A. Core Architecture and Data Model\nKafka organizes data into *topics*, each subdivided into multiple *partitions*. Producers write events to partitions sequentially, while consumers read them in the same order, enabling deterministic replay and horizontal scalability. A distributed coordination mechanism—initially ZooKeeper and now Kafka’s internal *KRaft controller*—manages partition metadata, broker membership, and leader election. Replication ensures fault tolerance, while batching and zero-copy I/O achieve high throughput.\nA key abstraction is the *log*, where each event is a time-ordered record identified by an offset. This append-only log model provides immutability and predictable performance. It also enables stream processors, such as Apache Flink, ksqlDB, or Kafka Streams, to perform stateful computations on continuous data without intermediate persistence layers.\n## B. Event Processing Semantics\nKafka supports three delivery guarantees—*at-most-once*, *at-least-once*, and *exactly-once*. The introduction of idempotent producers and transactional APIs (since Kafka 0.11) allows developers to build pipelines that maintain end-to-end consistency even under broker failures. Exactly-once semantics (EOS) have been adopted heavily in financial and compliance workloads, where duplicate or lost events are unacceptable [11], [18].\nIn large-scale deployments, Kafka often operates as an integration layer between OLTP and OLAP systems. For example, *Change Data Capture (CDC)* tools like Debezium stream row-level changes from relational databases into Kafka topics. Downstream consumers can materialize these streams into analytical stores or trigger reactive microservices, enabling near-real-time synchronization across systems.\n## C. Design Challenges in Event-Driven Architectures\nDespite its maturity, designing robust Kafka-based systems remains complex. Challenges include maintaining consistency across microservices, handling schema evolution, balancing throughput versus latency, and managing long-term retention. These challenges gave rise to recurring design patterns—such as CQRS, log compaction, and saga orchestration—that abstract proven architectural solutions [5], [6], [9].\nAnother persistent challenge involves benchmarking and reproducibility. Although standard suites like TPCx-Kafka provide a structured evaluation framework, variations in cluster configuration, message size, and partitioning make it difficult to compare published results objectively. The following sections address these gaps by analyzing both design patterns and benchmarking methodologies across forty-two representative studies.\n# Kafka Pattern Usage Heatmap\n&lt;img&gt;Kafka Pattern Usage Heatmap&lt;/img&gt;\nFig. 2. RELATED WORK\nSeveral studies have explored event-driven architectures and stream-processing frameworks, but few have synthesized reusable design patterns and benchmarking practices specifically for Kafka. Psaltoglou et al. [5] presented a systematic review of event-driven architectures for big data analytics but treated Kafka only as one of several messaging platforms. Berg [6] proposed stream-processing design patterns at a conceptual level, without empirical validation or benchmarking. Raptis and Passarella [18] provided a recent survey on networked data streaming, focusing primarily on performance modeling. In contrast, this study consolidates architecture-level design patterns, co-usage relationships, and reproducibility gaps across 42 Kafka-focused papers spanning multiple domains. This focus on reproducibility and empirical benchmarking distinguishes it from earlier literature.\n\n\n---\n\n\n## Page 3\n\nIV.
